title: Remembering Other People
---

A few months ago, I embarked on a journey to remember people’s names. Along the way, I learned a few good things.

If remembering names is trivial, there would not be over two million results on Google for “remembering names.” (Although there are twice as many results for “i can haz cheezburger”, which makes LOLcats at least twice as non-trivial.) Like many of my fellow humans, I could not remember 90 percent of the names that brush against my ears on the day-to-day. I simply accepted this fact as my tautology.

At some point, I decided things could be better. I started doing research into memory techniques. I read Joshua Foer’s Moonwalking with Einstein. I looked into the Memory Palace as a way of storing information. I approached every new person as an opportunity to practice.

I got better. I can remember most people’s names. Still, remembering names is hard. There are important things I learned:

Names are a sort of vocabulary for people. When you are equipped with a large vocabulary to describe the world around you, you see it with more fidelity than a person who knows less terms.

Consider the different shades of pink. Coral Pink (#F88379) is entirely different than Magenta (#FF00FF) or Rose (#FF007F). And Amaranth (#E52B50) is almost not pink at all. Having the vocabulary to describe the different shades of pink allows you to perceive and remember the world with higher resolution than simply having one umbrella term for “Pink”.

Similarly, remembering a person’s name gives you the same cool abilities. Suddenly people are not only “people”, they are Anna the programmer, or Ben from Wisconsin. With a larger vocabulary of names, people are individuals with their own thoughts and feelings. If you were like me, you probably already knew this, because you have close friends and close family. But now, like some kind of mental superman, you can juggle many more individuals. You can differentiate between them all.

When you read, “Rose”, did images of pink and red roses, Valentine’s Day, and the smell of spring and chocolates candies danced in your head? That’s how our mind works. You can imagine “Rose” and the associations conjured up by the term as key-room pairs where the key “Rose” opens up a room filled with Roses, Valentine’s Day, Spring, and Chocolates. An individual’s name is a key that unlocks the many associations you’ve had with her. Just remembering a name conjures up that memory of you two sharing a cold beer on a hill overlooking the soccer fields jeering the soccer tots and their moms. Or that time Buck made that inappropriate remark about his favorite past time at Thanksgiving dinner. When we remember names, we remember the individuals.

You may be thinking what’s all the fuss about. Why put in the effort. Practicing how to remember for the sake of remembering seems pointless. When I widened my mental model to encompass not just the people I see frequently, but to those I see in passing or sometimes, I find myself more concerned, more empathetic.

People tell stories about themselves. Most of the time, you get little snippets here and there. Putting together the little pieces of another human being’s personal narrative brings you closer to that individual. And when you feel closer to someone, you feel happy for some reason.
